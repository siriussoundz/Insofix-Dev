Spray Foam and Mortgage Problems

Spray foam can raise questions during a sale, purchase, remortgage or equity-release application. The right response depends on evidence, not pressure or assumptions.

Why lenders and surveyors ask questions

Foam may conceal parts of the roof structure and can make it harder to see timber, felt, membranes, ventilation paths and moisture condition. Some lenders may decline, request additional evidence, restrict valuation or ask for further investigation.

Policies vary and change

Lender, valuer and equity-release policies are not identical. HomeOwners Alliance reporting updated on 21 April 2026 referenced research suggesting around 250,000 UK homes may contain spray foam and that about 25% of mortgage lenders in that cited research were lending on affected properties. Treat those as dated research figures, not permanent rules, and confirm current requirements with your lender, broker, equity-release adviser or surveyor.

Paperwork helps but is not decisive

Installation certificates, product data, warranties and photographs can be useful, but missing, incomplete or unclear paperwork can make assessment harder and good paperwork does not automatically make a property acceptable to a lender.

Risks are property-specific

Poorly designed or inappropriate installations may restrict ventilation or contribute to trapped moisture, with possible damp, mould, condensation or timber-decay concerns. The presence of spray foam does not automatically mean that the roof has failed.

Removal is not an automatic answer

Removal should not be recommended without understanding foam type, roof construction, installation, accessible timber condition and the customer's objective. Removal alone does not guarantee mortgage, sale or equity-release acceptance.
